# WEB DESIGN DIRECTIVE

## 1. Core Purpose

Every website must be clean, responsive, SEO-friendly, and designed to convert visitors into calls, forms, bookings, consultations, purchases, or direct inquiries.

The website should answer three questions quickly:

1. What does this business do?
2. Why should the visitor trust this business?
3. What should the visitor do next?

If any section does not support clarity, trust, SEO, or conversion, simplify it or remove it.

---

## 2. Global Layout Rules

Use a full-width page structure with boxed inner content.

### Page Structure

* The page itself should be 100% wide.
* Each major section should span 100% width.
* Inner content should sit inside a centered container.
* Default max container width should be 1100px to 1200px.
* Use consistent horizontal padding so content never touches the screen edge.
* Use generous vertical padding so sections feel premium and breathable.

### Container Standard

Every section should follow this general pattern:

```html
<section class="section-name">
  <div class="container">
    Content goes here
  </div>
</section>
```

### Recommended Spacing

* Desktop section padding: 80px to 110px top and bottom.
* Tablet section padding: 64px to 80px top and bottom.
* Mobile section padding: 44px to 64px top and bottom.
* Container side padding: 20px to 32px.
* Card padding: 24px to 36px.
* Grid gap: 24px to 40px.

Avoid cramped layouts.

---

## 3. Responsive Design Rules

Design mobile-first.

Every page must work beautifully on mobile, tablet, laptop, and desktop.

### Responsive Requirements

* Columns must stack naturally on smaller screens.
* Buttons must be easy to tap.
* Text must remain readable.
* Cards must not feel squeezed.
* Images must scale without distortion.
* Navigation must be simple and usable.
* Forms must be easy to complete on mobile.
* Click-to-call buttons should be easy to access for local service businesses.
* No section should rely on desktop-only spacing or layout.

Mobile is not an afterthought. Mobile is the first battlefield.

---

## 4. Visual Design Rules

The design should feel clean, modern, trustworthy, spacious, and conversion-focused.

### Visual Standards

* Use strong contrast between text and background.
* Use clear typography with readable font sizes.
* Use consistent spacing.
* Use simple, balanced cards.
* Use clear visual hierarchy.
* Use brand colors intentionally.
* Use alternating section backgrounds when helpful.
* Use white, light gray, charcoal, or brand-tinted sections to create rhythm.
* Avoid clutter.
* Avoid tiny text.
* Avoid weak button contrast.
* Avoid decorative elements that do not support trust or conversion.
* Avoid generic stock-template energy.

The design should feel intentional, not busy.

---

## 5. SEO Rules

Every page must be structured for search engines and humans.

### Global SEO Requirements

* Use one clear H1 per page.
* Use logical H2 and H3 heading structure.
* Use keyword-aware headings naturally.
* Include service and location keywords where appropriate.
* Use descriptive image alt text.
* Use clean URLs.
* Use internal links between related pages.
* Use fast-loading sections.
* Avoid unnecessary scripts and heavy visual clutter.
* Write human-first content that search engines can understand.
* Include FAQ sections where useful.
* Include local trust signals when relevant.

### Metadata Requirements

Each page should have:

* SEO title.
* Meta description.
* Clear page slug.
* Open Graph title.
* Open Graph description.
* Relevant image alt text.
* Schema markup when appropriate.

Use LocalBusiness, Service, FAQPage, BreadcrumbList, and Organization schema when relevant.

---

## 6. Conversion Rules

Every page must guide the visitor toward action.

### CTA Standards

Each page should include:

* A primary CTA above the fold.
* A secondary CTA when useful.
* CTA repetition throughout the page.
* A final CTA before the footer.
* Mobile-friendly click-to-call where relevant.
* Forms that are simple and not overwhelming.

### CTA Examples

Use direct, action-based CTAs such as:

* Call Now
* Get a Free Quote
* Schedule a Consultation
* Request Service
* Book an Appointment
* Get Help Today
* Start Your Project
* Speak With Our Team

Avoid vague CTAs like:

* Learn More
* Submit
* Click Here

Use those only when they truly fit.

---

## 7. Trust Signal Rules

Trust must appear early and often.

### Trust Signals May Include

* Reviews.
* Testimonials.
* Star ratings.
* Years of experience.
* Certifications.
* Licenses.
* Awards.
* Case results.
* Before and after examples.
* Guarantees.
* Local service area.
* Client logos.
* Founder story.
* Team photos.
* Professional associations.
* Clear contact information.

The visitor should feel safe before being asked to act.

# PAGE-SPECIFIC DIRECTIVES

---

## 8. Homepage Directive

The homepage is the main conversion and trust page.

It should quickly explain what the business does, who it serves, why it is trustworthy, and what the visitor should do next.

### Homepage Structure

Use this section flow:

1. Navigation
2. Hero section
3. Trust bar
4. Problem section
5. Solution section
6. Services overview
7. Why choose us
8. Process section
9. Proof section
10. Service area or industries served
11. FAQ section
12. Final CTA
13. Footer

### Homepage Hero

The hero must include:

* Clear H1.
* Strong subheadline.
* Primary CTA.
* Secondary CTA when useful.
* Trust signal near the CTA.
* Optional supporting image or visual.
* Location or service area when relevant.

The hero should immediately tell the visitor what the business does.

### Homepage Services Overview

Show the main services as cards.

Each card should include:

* Service name.
* Short description.
* Link to the service page.
* Optional icon or image.

Do not overload the homepage with every detail. Use the homepage to route visitors deeper.

### Homepage Proof Section

Include reviews, testimonials, results, stats, or recognizable trust indicators.

The homepage must reduce doubt.

### Homepage Final CTA

The final CTA should summarize the offer and tell the visitor what to do next.

---

## 9. About Page Directive

The About page is a trust-building page, not a biography dump.

It should show who the business is, why it exists, what it values, and why customers can trust it.

### About Page Structure

Use this section flow:

1. About hero
2. Mission or purpose section
3. Founder or company story
4. Values section
5. Team or leadership section
6. Why customers trust us
7. Community or local connection
8. Proof or testimonials
9. Final CTA

### About Hero

The hero must include:

* Clear H1.
* Short trust-focused subheadline.
* Optional image of founder, team, office, or work environment.
* CTA to contact, book, or view services.

### Company Story

The story should be concise and relevant.

Focus on:

* Why the business exists.
* Who it helps.
* What problem it solves.
* What makes the approach different.
* Why the customer should feel confident.

Avoid long, self-centered copy.

### Values Section

Use 3 to 6 values.

Each value should include:

* Value title.
* Short explanation.
* Practical meaning for the customer.

Values should not sound generic. They should connect to customer trust.

### About Page CTA

End with a CTA that moves the visitor toward service.

Examples:

* Work With Us
* Schedule a Consultation
* Request a Quote
* See Our Services

---

## 10. Service Page Directive

A service page must rank for a specific service and convert visitors who need that service.

Each service page should focus on one main service.

### Service Page Structure

Use this section flow:

1. Service hero
2. Problem or pain section
3. Service explanation
4. Benefits section
5. What is included
6. Process section
7. Why choose us
8. Proof or testimonials
9. Related services
10. FAQ section
11. Final CTA

### Service Hero

The hero must include:

* Service-focused H1.
* Clear explanation of the service.
* Primary CTA.
* Secondary CTA when useful.
* Trust signal.
* Location modifier when relevant.

Example H1 patterns:

* Personal Injury Lawyer in [City]
* Emergency Plumbing Services in [City]
* Professional Web Design for Small Businesses
* Roof Repair Services in [City]

### Problem Section

Name the visitor’s pain clearly.

Explain what they may be dealing with and why it matters.

Do not overdramatize. Be direct and practical.

### Service Explanation

Explain the service in plain language.

The visitor should understand:

* What the service is.
* Who it is for.
* When they need it.
* What happens after they contact the business.
* What result they can expect.

### Benefits Section

Use benefit-driven cards.

Examples:

* Faster response.
* Clear pricing.
* Experienced help.
* Local service.
* Better results.
* Less stress.
* Professional handling.

### What Is Included

List concrete deliverables or service details.

This helps both SEO and conversion.

### Process Section

Use 3 to 5 simple steps.

Example:

1. Contact us.
2. Tell us what you need.
3. Get a clear plan.
4. We complete the work.
5. You get support after completion.

### Service FAQ

Include questions that buyers actually ask.

FAQs should target objections, pricing concerns, timing, process, qualifications, and local service questions.

### Service Page Final CTA

End with urgency and clarity.

Tell the visitor exactly how to start.

---

## 11. Location Page Directive

A location page must rank for a specific location and convert local visitors.

Each location page should focus on one city, town, neighborhood, county, or service area.

### Location Page Structure

Use this section flow:

1. Location hero
2. Local service overview
3. Services available in this location
4. Why choose us in this area
5. Local proof or trust signals
6. Nearby areas served
7. Local FAQ section
8. Final CTA

### Location Hero

The hero must include:

* Location-specific H1.
* Clear local service statement.
* Primary CTA.
* Click-to-call CTA when relevant.
* Trust signal.
* Mention of the city or service area.

Example H1 patterns:

* Web Design Services in [City]
* Personal Injury Lawyer Serving [City]
* Pest Control in [City]
* Roof Repair in [City]

### Local Service Overview

Explain that the business serves this area and what problems it solves there.

Do not create thin duplicate pages.

Each location page must have useful, location-aware content.

### Services Available in This Location

List relevant services available in the area.

Each service should link to its main service page when possible.

### Local Trust Section

Include local credibility when available.

Examples:

* Locally serving [City].
* Familiar with [City] homes, businesses, roads, climate, courts, neighborhoods, or customer needs.
* Reviews from nearby customers.
* Projects completed in or near the area.
* Local response times.
* Local team availability.

### Nearby Areas Served

Add nearby cities, towns, neighborhoods, or regions.

Use internal links when those pages exist.

### Location FAQ

Include local-intent questions.

Examples:

* Do you serve [City]?
* How quickly can you come to [City]?
* Do you offer free quotes in [City]?
* What services do you provide in [City]?
* Are you local to [City]?

### Location Page Final CTA

End with a direct local CTA.

Example:

Need help in [City]? Contact us today to schedule service, request a quote, or speak with our team.

# COMPONENT RULES

---

## 12. Navigation Rules

The navigation should be simple and conversion-focused.

### Navigation Should Include

* Logo.
* Main service links.
* About link.
* Location or service area link when relevant.
* Contact link.
* Primary CTA button.

### Navigation Rules

* Keep navigation clean.
* Avoid too many links.
* Use sticky navigation only when it improves usability.
* On mobile, use a clean menu.
* Make the primary CTA visible.
* For local services, include a phone CTA when useful.

---

## 13. Footer Rules

The footer should support trust, navigation, and SEO.

### Footer Should Include

* Logo.
* Short business description.
* Contact information.
* Address or service area when relevant.
* Phone number.
* Email.
* Main navigation links.
* Service links.
* Location links.
* Social links when available.
* Copyright.
* Privacy policy link.
* Terms link when needed.

The footer should not be an afterthought.

---

## 14. Card Rules

Cards should be clean, balanced, and easy to scan.

### Card Standards

* Use consistent padding.
* Use clear headings.
* Use short descriptions.
* Use icons or images only when helpful.
* Keep card heights visually balanced.
* Avoid crowded cards.
* Avoid too many cards in one row.
* On mobile, stack cards cleanly.

---

## 15. Button Rules

Buttons must be obvious and action-oriented.

### Button Standards

* Primary buttons should have strong contrast.
* Secondary buttons should be visible but less dominant.
* Button text should be specific.
* Buttons should have comfortable padding.
* Buttons should be easy to tap on mobile.
* Use consistent button styling across the site.

---

## 16. Form Rules

Forms should be simple.

### Form Standards

Only ask for what is necessary.

Common fields:

* Name.
* Email.
* Phone.
* Service needed.
* Message.

For local service businesses, phone number may be important.

Avoid long forms unless the business requires detailed intake.

---

## 17. Content Style Rules

Copy should be clear, confident, and human.

### Writing Standards

* Use plain language.
* Use short paragraphs.
* Use benefit-first headings.
* Avoid filler.
* Avoid fake hype.
* Avoid generic claims.
* Avoid keyword stuffing.
* Speak to the customer’s problem.
* Explain the next step clearly.

### Tone

The tone should be:

* Professional.
* Helpful.
* Clear.
* Trustworthy.
* Confident.
* Calm.

Do not sound robotic.

---

## 18. Internal Linking Rules

Every website should guide visitors and search engines through the site.

### Link Strategy

* Homepage links to main service pages.
* Homepage links to about and contact pages.
* Service pages link to related service pages.
* Service pages link to relevant location pages.
* Location pages link to service pages.
* Location pages link to nearby location pages.
* About page links to services and contact page.
* Footer links to priority pages.

Internal linking should feel natural.

---

## 19. Forbidden Design Choices

Do not create:

* Crowded layouts.
* Thin pages.
* Weak hero sections.
* Tiny text.
* Low contrast buttons.
* Overly decorative sections.
* Confusing navigation.
* Hidden CTAs.
* Long walls of text.
* Generic stock-template layouts.
* Duplicate location pages with only the city name changed.
* Desktop layouts that break on mobile.
* Forms that ask too much too soon.
* Pages without a clear conversion goal.

---

## 20. Build Principle

Every template should feel like a real business website that is ready to earn trust and generate leads.

The standard is simple:

Clean structure.
Strong SEO.
Responsive layout.
Clear copy.
Visible trust.
Obvious next step.

If the website does not help the visitor understand, trust, and act, improve the design before adding more content.

## Layout Framework Rules

Every website template must follow the same core layout system, even when the brand colors, images, typography, and visual style change.

The goal is not to make every website look identical. The goal is to make every website structurally consistent, easy to build, easy to edit, SEO-friendly, responsive, and conversion-focused.

### Global Layout Pattern

Every page should use this structure:

1. Full-width outer sections
2. Centered boxed inner containers
3. Consistent max-width
4. Consistent spacing scale
5. Predictable section order
6. Reusable grid patterns
7. Reusable CTA placement
8. Reusable trust placement
9. Reusable mobile stacking behavior

### Standard Section Formula

Each section should follow this layout pattern:

1. Section wrapper
2. Inner container
3. Section eyebrow or label when useful
4. Section headline
5. Section intro text
6. Content grid, card group, media block, or CTA
7. Optional supporting link or button

### Container Rules

* Outer section width: 100%
* Inner container max-width: 1100px to 1200px
* Inner container alignment: centered
* Standard desktop side padding: 32px
* Standard tablet side padding: 24px
* Standard mobile side padding: 20px
* No content should touch the browser edge
* No major section should ignore the container system

### Layout Types

Use these reusable layout types across all templates:

#### 1. Hero Layout

Use for the top of important pages.

Pattern:

* Left side: headline, subheadline, CTA, trust signal
* Right side: image, form, testimonial card, stat card, or visual proof
* Mobile: stack text first, visual second

#### 2. Centered Intro Layout

Use when introducing a page or major section.

Pattern:

* Eyebrow
* Centered headline
* Centered short paragraph
* Optional CTA below

#### 3. Two-Column Content Layout

Use for explanation, story, benefits, and service detail sections.

Pattern:

* One side: headline and copy
* Other side: image, list, card, stat, or supporting proof
* Mobile: stack cleanly

#### 4. Card Grid Layout

Use for services, benefits, values, features, FAQs, and location lists.

Pattern:

* Section intro
* 2, 3, or 4 column grid
* Cards with consistent padding
* Mobile: stack into one column

#### 5. Process Layout

Use for steps.

Pattern:

* Section intro
* 3 to 5 numbered steps
* Each step includes title and short explanation
* Optional CTA after steps

#### 6. Proof Layout

Use for testimonials, reviews, stats, case studies, awards, and trust markers.

Pattern:

* Section intro
* Review cards, stat row, logos, or testimonial slider
* CTA or contact prompt when useful

#### 7. CTA Band Layout

Use between major sections and before the footer.

Pattern:

* Full-width background
* Boxed inner content
* Strong headline
* Short support copy
* Primary CTA
* Optional secondary CTA

#### 8. FAQ Layout

Use near the bottom of service, location, and homepage templates.

Pattern:

* Section intro
* Accordion or stacked FAQ list
* Questions written with SEO and buyer objections in mind
* CTA after FAQs

### Page Layout Consistency

The same page types should follow the same structural order.

3. Problem
4. Service explanation
5. Benefits grid
6. What is included
7. Process
8. Why choose us
9. Proof
10. Related services
11. FAQ
12. Final CTA
13. Footer

#### Location Page Layout Framework

1. Nav
2. Location hero
3. Local service overview
4. Services in this area
5. Why choose us locally
6. Local proof
7. Nearby areas served
8. Local FAQ
9. Final CTA
10. Footer

### Spacing System

Use a consistent spacing scale across all pages.

Recommended spacing scale:

* XS: 8px
* SM: 16px
* MD: 24px
* LG: 40px
* XL: 64px
* XXL: 96px

Use this spacing system for:

* Section padding
* Card padding
* Grid gaps
* Text spacing
* CTA spacing
* Form spacing

### Grid System

Use predictable grids.

* 2-column grids for explanations and image/text sections
* 3-column grids for services, benefits, and values
* 4-column grids only when content is very short
* 1-column layouts for mobile
* Avoid odd, unstable layouts unless specifically requested

### Mobile Stacking Rules

Mobile layout should follow this order:

1. Headline
2. Supporting text
3. CTA
4. Trust signal
5. Image or supporting visual
6. Cards or details

Never place decorative visuals above the core message on mobile unless they are essential.

### Template Principle

Templates may change visually, but the layout logic should remain consistent.

Different brand. Same bones.
Different colors. Same conversion path.
Different niche. Same trust-building structure.
Different content. Same responsive framework.

# GOVERNANCE, QA, AND CORRECTION RULES

These rules control how the website should be built, maintained, corrected, improved, and measured.

The goal is not only to create clean websites. The goal is to create websites that can be edited safely without unnecessary rewrites, layout damage, visual drift, or accidental breakage.

---

## 21. Governance Layer

Every template must have a clear governance system.

Governance means the website has rules for:

1. Layout consistency
2. Component behavior
3. Spacing
4. Typography
5. SEO
6. Accessibility
7. Performance
8. Analytics
9. Conversion tracking
10. Quality assurance
11. Correction behavior
12. Safe editing

A website should not be treated as a loose collection of pages. It should be treated as a system.

Every page, section, card, button, form, image, and CTA should obey the same structural logic.

The design may change visually from client to client, but the operating rules should remain consistent.

---

## 22. Component Governance Rules

Every website should use reusable component logic.

Components should be predictable, consistent, and easy to edit.

### Required Component Categories

Every template should define or support these reusable component types:

1. Navigation
2. Hero section
3. Trust bar
4. Section intro
5. Service card
6. Benefit card
7. Process step
8. Testimonial card
9. Review block
10. FAQ item
11. CTA band
12. Contact form
13. Footer
14. Location card
15. Related service card
16. Feature list
17. Image and text block
18. Stat block
19. Logo bar
20. Breadcrumb block

### Component Consistency

Components should share consistent:

* Padding
* Border radius
* Typography scale
* Button placement
* Icon sizing
* Card spacing
* Heading hierarchy
* Mobile stacking behavior
* Hover states
* Focus states
* Alignment rules

A service card on the homepage should feel related to a related-service card on a service page. They do not need to be identical, but they should feel like they belong to the same system.

### Component Variants

Components may have variants, but variants must be intentional.

Examples:

* Light card
* Dark card
* Featured card
* Compact card
* Horizontal card
* Icon card
* Image card

Do not create a new component style every time a new section appears. Reuse existing patterns whenever possible.

### Component Rule

If a new design element can be made from an existing component with a small variant, use the existing component system.

Do not invent a new structure unless the existing structure cannot serve the content.

---

6. Border radius
7. Shadows
8. Container widths
9. Breakpoints
10. Button sizes

12. Card padding

### Spacing Tokens

Use a predictable spacing system.

Recommended scale:

* 4px

* 12px
* 16px
* 20px
* 24px
* 32px
* 40px
* 48px
* 64px
* 80px
* 96px

Recommended:

* Small container: 760px to 860px
* Standard container: 1100px to 1200px
* Wide container: 1280px to 1360px

Use a readable type system.

Each template should define:

* H1 size
* H2 size
* H3 size
* Body size
* Small text size
* Button text size
* Line height for headings
* Line height for body text

Avoid one-off font sizes across random sections.

## 24. Accessibility Rules

Every website must be accessible enough for real users, not just visually attractive.

Accessibility is part of quality. It is not optional decoration.

### Semantic HTML

Use proper semantic structure.

Required:

* One H1 per page
* Logical H2 and H3 hierarchy
* Proper button elements for actions
* Proper anchor elements for links
* Proper label elements for form fields
* Proper list elements for lists
* Proper sectioning where appropriate

Do not use divs for everything when semantic HTML would be better.

### Text Contrast

Text must be readable.

* Body text must have strong contrast.
* CTA buttons must be easy to read.
* Light gray text on white backgrounds should be avoided.
* Dark text on dark backgrounds should be avoided.
* Brand colors must not reduce readability.

### Keyboard Navigation

Interactive elements should be usable with keyboard navigation.

Required:

* Buttons must be focusable.
* Links must be focusable.
* Forms must be usable by keyboard.
* Dropdowns and mobile menus must be accessible.
* Focus states must be visible.

### Images and Alt Text

Every important image should have useful alt text.

Alt text should describe the image in context.

Examples:

* "Personal injury attorney meeting with client in office"
* "Roof repair crew inspecting storm damage"
* "Web design dashboard showing local business website"

Decorative images may use empty alt text if they do not add meaning.

### Forms

Forms must be accessible.

Required:

* Every field must have a label.
* Required fields should be clear.
* Error states should be visible.
* Success states should be clear.
* Form buttons should describe the action.
* Avoid placeholder-only labels.

### Accessibility Principle

A visitor should be able to understand, navigate, read, and act on the website without fighting the interface.

---

## 25. Performance Rules

Every website must load quickly and feel lightweight.

Performance affects SEO, conversion, and trust.

### Performance Standards

The site should avoid unnecessary bloat.

Do not add heavy scripts, large animations, oversized images, unused libraries, or decorative effects that slow the website down without improving trust or conversion.

### Image Performance

Images should be optimized.

Required:

* Use modern formats when possible.
* Compress images.
* Avoid uploading massive original files.
* Use responsive image sizing.
* Lazy-load below-the-fold images when appropriate.
* Do not use background videos unless explicitly requested.
* Do not use huge hero images that slow the first load.

### Script Performance

Scripts should be minimal.

Avoid:

* Unnecessary animation libraries
* Unused tracking scripts
* Bloated sliders
* Heavy third-party widgets
* Excessive embedded content

### Layout Stability

The page should not jump around while loading.

Required:

* Reserve image dimensions.
* Avoid layout shift.
* Avoid late-loading sections that push content down.
* Keep above-the-fold content stable.

### Performance Principle

Fast feels professional. Slow feels untrustworthy.

Every website should define what success means.

A website is not finished until it can measure visitor actions.

### Primary Conversion Events

Each website should identify the primary conversion goal.

Examples:

* Phone call
* Contact form submission
* Booking request
* Consultation request
* Quote request
* Purchase
* Email click
* Map click
* Chat interaction

### Secondary Conversion Events

Secondary conversion events may include:

* Click-to-call
* Click-to-email
* Contact page visit
* Service page visit
* Location page visit
* FAQ interaction
* Scroll depth
* CTA click
* Download click
* Appointment button click
* Directions click

### Tracking Requirements

Where possible, track:

* Primary CTA clicks
* Secondary CTA clicks
* Form submissions
* Phone number clicks
* Email clicks
* Booking link clicks
* Location or map clicks
* Navigation clicks to service pages
* Final CTA clicks

### CTA Naming

CTA buttons should have clear tracking names.

Examples:

* hero_call_now
* hero_get_quote
* nav_schedule_consultation
* service_page_contact_form
* final_cta_request_service

### Conversion Rule

If a button matters, it should be trackable.

If a form matters, it should be trackable.

If a phone click matters, it should be trackable.

### Measurement Principle

A website should not merely exist. It should report whether it is helping the business earn calls, leads, bookings, or sales.

## 30. Correction and Safe Editing Rules

Corrections must be surgical.

When the user requests a specific adjustment, only adjust the requested item.

Do not rewrite unrelated files.
Do not redesign unrelated sections.
Do not refactor unrelated components.
Do not change global styling unless explicitly requested.
Do not rename classes unless necessary.
Do not restructure the page unless requested.
Do not alter copy unless requested.
Do not improve things the user did not ask to improve.

### The Sauce Dab Rule

If the user says there is sauce on their face, dab the sauce.

Do not throw the whole person into the shower.

In website terms:

If the user says one card in one section needs adjusting, adjust that card in that section.

Do not rebuild the page.
Do not rewrite the component system.
Do not change every card.
Do not alter the whole grid.
Do not redesign the brand.
Do not change the spacing scale.
Do not touch unrelated pages.

Small problem. Small correction.

### Correction Scope Rule

Every correction must identify the smallest safe scope.

Correction scope should follow this order:

1. Exact text
2. Exact class
3. Exact element
4. Exact card
5. Exact section
6. Exact component instance
7. Shared component
8. Page-level layout
9. Global styles
10. Full template system

Always choose the smallest scope that solves the problem.

Do not escalate the scope unless the correction truly requires it.

### User Request Priority

If the user says:

* "Adjust this card"
* "Fix this button"
* "Change the padding here"
* "Move this section"
* "Make this headline smaller"
* "This specific card needs more spacing"
* "This one section feels too cramped"

Then only that target should be changed.

The rest of the website should remain untouched.

### No Panic Refactor Rule

Do not enter panic mode.

A correction request is not permission to refactor the project.

Avoid unnecessary edits such as:

* Rebuilding all components
* Reformatting entire files
* Changing unrelated class names
* Updating unrelated pages
* Replacing layout systems
* Moving content across files
* Changing global design tokens
* Altering unrelated responsive rules

Only solve the stated problem.

### Diff Discipline Rule

When making changes, keep the diff small.

A good correction should usually produce a small, understandable change.

If a correction creates a large diff, it must be justified by the request.

For small visual adjustments, prefer changing:

* One class
* One style rule
* One component prop
* One spacing value
* One layout wrapper
* One responsive rule
* One content block

Do not modify large blocks of unrelated code.

### Preserve Existing Intent

When correcting one issue, preserve the current design intent.

Do not change:

* Brand colors
* Typography system
* Page structure
* CTA strategy
* SEO headings
* Component names
* Layout order
* Content hierarchy
* Tracking names
* Schema
* Navigation
* Footer

Unless the user explicitly asks for those things.

### Read Before Editing

Before making a correction, first understand where the problem lives.

Identify:

1. The page
2. The section
3. The component
4. The exact element
5. The relevant class or style
6. The smallest necessary change

Do not edit blindly.

### One Request, One Correction

If the user gives one correction, make one correction.

Do not bundle extra improvements unless the user asks.

Examples:

User says:
"The service cards need more padding."

Correct response:
Increase padding on the service cards only.

Incorrect response:
Change the entire services section, alter colors, rewrite service copy, change icons, and modify the homepage layout.

User says:
"The hero button should be more visible."

Correct response:
Adjust the hero button contrast or size.

Incorrect response:
Rewrite the hero, change the background image, alter the navigation, and update all buttons sitewide.

User says:
"The third card feels off."

Correct response:
Adjust the third card only.

Incorrect response:
Rebuild the entire card grid.

### Shared Component Caution

If the target is part of a shared component, be careful.

Before editing a shared component, determine whether the change should affect:

1. Only one instance
2. All instances on one page
3. All instances across the site

If the user only mentions one instance, do not change every instance.

If needed, add a local class, prop, variant, or wrapper to target only the requested item.

### Global Style Caution

Do not change global styles unless the user asks for a global change.

Global styles affect the whole site and can create unintended damage.

Avoid touching:

* Root variables
* Global typography
* Global spacing
* Body styles
* Container styles
* Button defaults
* Card defaults
* Breakpoints
* Theme colors

Unless the correction clearly requires it.

### Correction Confirmation Format

After making a correction, summarize only what changed.

The summary should be short and specific.

Good:

* "Adjusted the padding on the service cards in the homepage services section."
* "Increased the contrast of the hero CTA button only."
* "Reduced spacing inside the third testimonial card."
* "Updated the mobile stacking for the location cards."

Bad:

* "I improved the whole page."
* "I cleaned up the codebase."
* "I refactored the design system."
* "I made several enhancements."

If several enhancements were not requested, do not make them.

### Correction Principle

Correction work should feel calm, precise, and controlled.

A requested tweak is a scalpel job, not a demolition permit.

* Never expose private customer data in source code, logs, analytics, URLs, or client-side storage.

### Secrets and Credentials

* Never commit API keys, passwords, tokens, private certificates, or service credentials.
* Use environment variables or an approved secret manager.
* Provide example environment files with placeholders only.
* Keep privileged operations on the server.
* Rotate any credential that was accidentally exposed.
* Use separate credentials and access levels for development, staging, and production when practical.

### Forms and Input

* Validate input on the server even when client validation exists.
* Sanitize or safely encode untrusted content before rendering or storing it.
* Protect public forms from spam and automated abuse with the least intrusive effective control.
* Apply rate limits to sensitive or abuse-prone actions.
* Use CSRF protection where the architecture requires it.
* Restrict uploads by type, size, and purpose; never trust filenames or browser-provided MIME types alone.
* Return clear user errors without exposing stack traces, credentials, database details, or internal paths.

### Browser and Transport Security

* Production websites must use HTTPS.
* Use secure, HttpOnly, and appropriate SameSite cookie settings for sensitive cookies.
* Apply a practical Content Security Policy when compatible with the site.
* Set relevant security headers, including protections against MIME sniffing and unauthorized framing.
* Do not use `target="_blank"` without safe opener behavior.
* Avoid unsafe HTML injection. If raw HTML is unavoidable, sanitize it with a proven library.

### Authentication and Authorization

* Use established authentication systems rather than inventing custom password handling.
* Enforce authorization on the server for every protected action.
* Apply least privilege to users, services, databases, and integrations.
* Do not rely on hidden UI elements as access control.
* Protect administrative routes and sensitive operations with stronger controls than public pages.

### Dependencies and Integrations

* Prefer maintained dependencies with a clear purpose.
* Do not add a package when the platform or existing stack already solves the need safely.
* Remove unused packages, scripts, trackers, and third-party widgets.
* Review material security advisories before launch and during maintenance.
* Limit third-party scripts because they affect security, privacy, performance, and reliability.
* Give integrations only the scopes and permissions they need.

### Privacy and Consent

* Explain what data is collected, why it is collected, and who receives it.
* Obtain consent where required before activating nonessential tracking or marketing technologies.
* Do not use deceptive consent interfaces or preselected optional consent.
* Honor applicable opt-out, deletion, correction, and access requests.
* Avoid sending personal or sensitive data to analytics platforms.
* Configure analytics for privacy-conscious collection and retention.
* Provide a clear contact method for privacy questions.

### Logging and Monitoring

* Log enough to diagnose failures and abuse without logging secrets or unnecessary personal data.
* Redact sensitive fields.
* Monitor critical form delivery, authentication, payment, and integration failures.
* Use actionable error reporting without exposing internals to visitors.

### Security Principle

No launch is complete if the site works visually but leaks data, exposes credentials, trusts unvalidated input, or grants excessive access.

## 35. Content and Design Source Authority

When sources conflict, use this priority order unless David explicitly changes it:

1. David's latest explicit instruction
2. Applicable legal, security, privacy, and accessibility requirements
3. Approved brand card, mockup, or source-of-truth design asset
4. Verified client facts and approved business records
5. Existing project directives and project-level instructions
6. Existing production content and behavior
7. Conservative placeholders or clearly labeled assumptions

Never resolve missing facts by inventing claims.

When a conflict cannot be resolved safely, preserve working content and identify the exact decision needed.

## 36. Launch Readiness Checklist

Before production launch, verify:

### Domain and Delivery

* Production domain resolves correctly.
* HTTPS and certificate coverage work for required hostnames.
* Preferred hostname redirects consistently.
* HTTP redirects to HTTPS.
* Required legacy URLs use intentional redirects.
* No redirect loops or unnecessary redirect chains exist.
* Custom error pages or framework fallbacks work appropriately.

### Search and GEO

* Production pages are indexable as intended.
* Canonical URLs point to the correct production URLs.
* `robots.txt` is production-safe.
* XML sitemap exists, is accurate, and uses canonical URLs.
* No staging domain, preview URL, or placeholder hostname appears in metadata, schema, links, or sitemap files.
* Titles, descriptions, Open Graph data, schema, headings, alt text, and internal links are validated.

### Function and Conversion

* Forms submit successfully and reach the intended recipient or system.
* Success and error states work.
* Phone, email, booking, map, payment, and primary CTA links work.
* A broken-link check covers internal links, external priority links, images, scripts, stylesheets, canonical URLs, and sitemap URLs.
* Conversion events fire once with useful names and no sensitive payloads.
* Spam controls work without blocking normal users.
* Navigation, menus, accordions, dialogs, and interactive controls work by touch and keyboard.

### Production Hygiene

* Placeholder text, test content, debug output, test accounts, and dead links are removed.
* Production environment variables are configured without exposing secrets.
* Analytics, consent, and privacy behavior match the approved plan.
* Backup and rollback paths exist before launch.
* A post-launch smoke test is completed on the live domain.

## 37. Maintenance and Operational Rules

Every production site must have a practical ownership and maintenance path.

Define:

* Who owns the domain, hosting, analytics, forms, integrations, and content approvals.
* Who owns uptime monitoring, alert response, incident coordination, and restoration decisions.
* How form delivery and critical failures are monitored.
* How backups are created, retained, and restored.
* How dependencies and security advisories are reviewed.
* How content, offers, staff details, hours, locations, and legal pages are kept current.
* How production changes are approved and rolled back.

### Maintenance Cadence

At an appropriate recurring interval:

* Confirm the site is reachable and HTTPS is valid.
* Review uptime checks and investigate recurring availability or latency failures.
* Test the primary conversion path.
* Review form delivery failures and spam volume.
* Check broken links and crawl errors.
* Review dependency and platform security notices.
* Confirm backups can be restored, not merely created.
* Review analytics and conversion tracking for unexpected gaps.
* Remove obsolete scripts, users, integrations, and permissions.

Maintenance frequency should match business risk and change volume. Critical commerce, legal, medical, financial, or high-lead-value sites require tighter monitoring.

## 38. Legal and Compliance Readiness

Legal requirements vary by jurisdiction, industry, audience, and data flow. Do not present template language as legal advice.

Where applicable, provide or obtain approved versions of:

* Privacy policy
* Terms of use or service
* Cookie or tracking notice
* Accessibility statement
* Refund, cancellation, shipping, or return policies
* Professional, medical, legal, financial, affiliate, testimonial, or advertising disclaimers
* Required business identity and contact disclosures

Legal links should be accessible from the footer or relevant interaction point.

Consent records, marketing opt-ins, and policy effective dates should be handled according to applicable requirements. Claims and testimonials must be accurate, supportable, and approved.

## 39. Core Web Vitals and Performance Targets

Use current official Web Vitals definitions and measurement guidance at implementation time.

Unless a stricter project target applies, aim for the current "good" thresholds at the 75th percentile of real-user mobile and desktop visits:

* Largest Contentful Paint: 2.5 seconds or less
* Interaction to Next Paint: 200 milliseconds or less
* Cumulative Layout Shift: 0.1 or less

Also verify:

* Above-the-fold content is useful before nonessential scripts finish.
* Images have appropriate dimensions, formats, sizes, and loading behavior.
* Fonts do not create avoidable blocking or visual instability.
* Third-party code has a demonstrated business purpose.
* Performance is tested on production-like builds, not inferred from development mode.

If a target cannot be met, document the measured result, primary cause, business tradeoff, and smallest corrective action.

## 40. Browser and Device Support

Support the current stable releases of major evergreen browsers and the mobile browsers used by the target audience, unless project analytics justify a different matrix.

At minimum, verify representative behavior in:

* Chromium-based desktop browser
* Safari desktop when relevant
* Firefox desktop
* iOS Safari
* Android Chrome
* Small mobile viewport
* Tablet viewport
* Standard laptop viewport
* Wide desktop viewport

The site must remain readable and usable when optional effects, animations, advanced CSS, or JavaScript enhancements are unavailable. Critical content and conversion paths require graceful degradation.

## 41. Content Freshness Rules

Time-sensitive content must have an owner and review path.

Review and update:

* Business hours, addresses, phone numbers, and service areas
* Staff, credentials, licenses, and professional affiliations
* Prices, offers, deadlines, availability, and guarantees
* Statistics, citations, laws, platform instructions, and industry claims
* Copyright years only when the implementation requires manual maintenance
* Schema, metadata, FAQs, and location details when visible facts change

Do not display false "last updated" dates. Preserve meaningful publication and modification dates when content systems support them.

Remove, redirect, archive, or clearly label obsolete content according to search value and user need. Do not leave stale pages that mislead buyers or AI systems.

## 42. Deployment Governance

Production changes must be deliberate, reviewable, and reversible.

### Required Deployment Controls

* Use a staging, preview, or equivalent validation environment for material changes when available.
* Keep environment-specific configuration separate from source code.
* Run the smallest relevant automated checks before deployment.
* Review the actual diff and confirm only intended files changed.
* Require human approval from the designated owner before material production changes unless an approved emergency procedure applies.
* Do not deploy unreviewed production changes.
* Protect production branches and credentials according to project risk.
* Do not use production customer data in development unless explicitly authorized and properly protected.
* Record material migrations, configuration changes, and rollback steps.
* Avoid high-risk releases when no responsible person can verify or recover the site.

### Post-Deployment Verification

After deployment, verify the live production URL, primary conversion path, forms, tracking, metadata, schema, responsive layout, and key integrations.

If a deployment causes material harm, restore service first using the safest rollback or forward fix. Diagnose broader improvements after stability returns.

## 43. Definition of Done

A website build or material update is complete only when the applicable checks below pass:

* Requested scope is fully implemented without unrelated changes.
* Approved brand assets and mockups are faithfully represented.
* Responsive behavior works across required viewports.
* Navigation, links, forms, and conversion paths work.
* Accessibility checks cover semantics, keyboard use, focus, labels, contrast, and meaningful alternatives.
* SEO and GEO checks cover metadata, crawlability, canonicals, sitemap, robots, schema, entity language, FAQs, and internal links.
* Security and privacy checks cover secrets, input handling, permissions, data collection, consent, and third parties.
* Performance is measured against the project's targets.
* Analytics and conversion events are verified without collecting prohibited data.
* Legal and policy requirements are present or explicitly assigned for approval.
* Production configuration, backups, monitoring, and rollback are ready.
* The live deployment passes a post-launch smoke test.
* No fake claims, reviews, credentials, statistics, locations, or authority signals were introduced.
* Known exceptions are documented with their reason, risk, and owner.

### Final Confidence Rule

Confidence must come from evidence, not tone.

Do not claim a site is secure, compliant, accessible, fast, indexed, or conversion-ready without the relevant verification. Report what was checked, what passed, what remains unverified, and the main residual risk.
